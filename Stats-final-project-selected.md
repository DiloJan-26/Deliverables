# 7. Suggested hypotheses

## Hypothesis 1 — Employment status

**H0:** There is no significant difference in self-regulated learning scores between employed and non-employed postgraduate computing students.
**H1:** There is a significant difference in self-regulated learning scores between employed and non-employed postgraduate computing students.

Test:

> Independent samples t-test

---

## Hypothesis 2 — AI-tool usage

**H0:** Self-regulated learning scores do not significantly differ across AI-tool usage frequency groups.
**H1:** At least one AI-tool usage group has a significantly different self-regulated learning score.

Test:

> One-way ANOVA

---

## Hypothesis 3 — Predictors of self-regulated learning

**H0:** Self-efficacy, task value, effort regulation, and time management do not significantly predict overall self-regulated learning.
**H1:** At least one of these factors significantly predicts overall self-regulated learning.

Test:

> Multiple linear regression

# 11. Practical difficulties and smart solutions

## Difficulty 1 — Not enough postgraduate responses

### Problem

Postgraduate DS/AI students may be limited.

### Smart solution

Use the broader but still valid target:

> postgraduate computing students in DS/AI-related programmes

Include CS, SE, IT, Data Analytics, and AI-related programmes.

This protects your sample size without damaging the topic.

---

## Difficulty 2 — Questionnaire too long

### Problem

Students may ignore a long survey.

### Smart solution

Keep the main MSLQ section to **24–30 items**.

Tell respondents:

> This survey will take approximately 5–7 minutes.

Short surveys get better completion rates.

---

## Difficulty 3 — Weak validation

### Problem

Only doing Cronbach’s Alpha may look basic.

### Smart solution

Use a validation triangle:

1. **Expert review** — content validity
2. **Cronbach’s Alpha** — internal consistency
3. **Factor analysis** — construct validity
4. **Test-retest reliability** — stability, if time allows

That looks much more master’s-level.

---

## Difficulty 4 — Factor analysis may be weak with small sample

### Problem

Factor analysis needs enough responses.

### Smart solution

Keep the number of items controlled.

Use:

> 24–27 items and at least 120–150 responses.

Also report:

* KMO value
* Bartlett’s test
* factor loadings
* variance explained

Even a clean exploratory factor analysis will improve your paper.

---

## Difficulty 5 — Students give careless answers

### Problem

Some respondents select the same option for everything.

### Smart solution

Add one attention-check item, such as:

> To confirm attention, please select “Agree” for this item.

Use carefully. Do not overuse it.

Also check response time. If someone completes 40 questions in 30 seconds, consider removing that response.
