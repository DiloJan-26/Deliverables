# 2. Why PSS is easier

PSS is easier because it measures one main idea: **perceived stress**.

So your analysis is straightforward:

> stress score → compare groups → test predictors of stress

Example:

* working vs non-working students
* male vs female students
* high workload vs low workload
* sleep hours vs stress

The questionnaire is also usually shorter, so respondents finish quickly. Scoring and interpretation are easier.

That is why PSS is safer.

---

# 3. Why MSLQ is more difficult

MSLQ is harder because it does not measure only one thing. It measures **motivation plus learning strategies**. That means your questionnaire may involve multiple dimensions such as:

* intrinsic motivation
* extrinsic motivation
* task value
* self-efficacy
* test anxiety
* self-regulation
* effort regulation
* time management
* help-seeking
* study strategies

This creates four main difficulties.

---

## Difficulty 1 — It can become too long

The full MSLQ is usually much longer than PSS. If your Google Form is too long, students may:

* stop halfway
* answer carelessly
* select the same option repeatedly
* avoid participating

### How to tackle this

Do not use the entire questionnaire unless required.

Use a **focused adapted version** with selected MSLQ sections that match your research topic.

For example, choose only:

1. **Self-efficacy for learning**
2. **Task value**
3. **Self-regulation**
4. **Effort regulation**
5. **Time/study management**

This makes the project manageable and still meaningful.

But be honest in the report:

> This study adapted selected MSLQ subscales relevant to postgraduate Data Science and AI learning.

---

## Difficulty 2 — Validation is more complex

For PSS, Cronbach’s Alpha for the whole scale may be enough, plus maybe a simple factor analysis.

For MSLQ, because there are multiple dimensions, you should calculate reliability separately for each subscale.

Example:

| Subscale          | Cronbach’s Alpha |
| ----------------- | ---------------- |
| Self-efficacy     | 0.82             |
| Task value        | 0.78             |
| Self-regulation   | 0.75             |
| Effort regulation | 0.71             |

This is more work, but it looks more master’s-level.

The project specifically expects validation through methods such as **Cronbach’s Alpha, factor analysis, and test-retest reliability**. 

### How to tackle this

Keep only **4–5 subscales**. Do not make 10 different dimensions.

That way, validation is still impressive but not overwhelming.

---

## Difficulty 3 — Factor analysis needs enough responses

If you use many items, factor analysis needs more data.

A long questionnaire with many items but only 50 responses will look weak.

### How to tackle this

Use around **20–30 questionnaire items maximum**.

Target at least:

* **30 responses for pilot**
* **100–150 responses for main survey**

For 12 days, this is achievable if your group shares the form actively.

---

## Difficulty 4 — The research question must be focused

If you say:

> We study motivation and learning strategies among all university students

that is too broad.

But if you say:

> We study motivation and self-regulated learning among postgraduate Data Science and AI students

that is focused and strong.

### How to tackle this

Make the topic specific to your degree programme.

Do not make the project about general education. Make it about **AI/Data Science postgraduate learning**.

# 5. Smart hacks to make MSLQ achievable

## Hack 1 — Do not use the full MSLQ

Use a focused version with selected subscales.

Recommended structure:

| Construct             | Why include it?                                                 |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Task value            | Measures whether students see Data Science/AI modules as useful |
| Self-efficacy         | Measures confidence in handling AI/statistics/programming work  |
| Self-regulation       | Measures ability to plan and control learning                   |
| Effort regulation     | Measures persistence when coursework is difficult               |
| Time/study management | Measures ability to handle workload and deadlines               |

This gives you a strong Data Science and AI academic angle.

---

## Hack 2 — Keep the questionnaire below 30 items

A good structure:

| Section                        | Number of questions |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------: |
| Consent                        |                   1 |
| Eligibility                    |                   1 |
| Demographics                   |                 6–8 |
| MSLQ-adapted items             |               24–28 |
| Optional AI learning questions |                 3–5 |

Total should be around **35–40 questions including demographics**, but the main Likert scale should stay around **24–28 items**.

---

## Hack 3 — Use a 5-point Likert scale

Use:

1. Strongly disagree
2. Disagree
3. Neutral
4. Agree
5. Strongly agree

This is easy for respondents and easy for analysis.

---

## Hack 4 — Use simple analysis, but present it well

For MSLQ, you can do:

1. Descriptive statistics
2. Cronbach’s Alpha for each subscale
3. Exploratory Factor Analysis
4. Independent samples t-test
5. One-way ANOVA
6. Correlation
7. Multiple linear regression

The project expects descriptive and inferential statistics such as t-tests, ANOVA, and regression analysis. 

---

## Hack 5 — Make one strong dependent variable

To avoid confusion, create one main outcome variable:

> **Self-regulated learning score**

Then test what predicts it.

Example predictors:

* AI-tool usage frequency
* employment status
* weekly study hours
* prior programming experience
* confidence in mathematics/statistics
* mode of learning: online, physical, hybrid

This makes your analysis clean.

# 10. Suggested hypotheses

Use 3 main hypotheses only. Do not overload the project.

### Hypothesis 1 — Employment status

**H0:** There is no significant difference in self-regulated learning scores between employed and non-employed postgraduate Data Science and AI students.
**H1:** There is a significant difference in self-regulated learning scores between employed and non-employed postgraduate Data Science and AI students.

Test: **Independent samples t-test**

---

### Hypothesis 2 — AI-tool usage

**H0:** Self-regulated learning scores do not differ significantly across AI-tool usage frequency groups.
**H1:** At least one AI-tool usage frequency group has a significantly different self-regulated learning score.

Test: **One-way ANOVA**

---

### Hypothesis 3 — Predictors of learning strategy

**H0:** Self-efficacy, task value, and effort regulation do not significantly predict overall learning strategy score.
**H1:** At least one of self-efficacy, task value, or effort regulation significantly predicts overall learning strategy score.

Test: **Multiple linear regression**
